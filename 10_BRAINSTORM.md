## Open question 1 — What is our Harvard?

Facebook was Harvard. Stripe was seven developers who hated PayPal. Shopify was one snowboard shop in Ottawa. Airbnb was three air mattresses at SXSW.

**What is ours?**

Three candidates:
1. **Orchid obsessives (OKG wedge)** — a passionate, global, media-ready community. Ecuagenera's customer list is our first dorm. AOS judges are our second. Botanical illustrators, taxonomists, specialist growers round it out. Highest emotional yield. Lowest revenue per user.
2. **Small healthcare clinics doing credentialing (HKG wedge)** — John's direct network. $500–$5K/month per clinic. 1,000 orgs in John's existing relationships. Fastest revenue per user. Lowest emotional yield.
3. **AI developers building vertical agents (Machine Lane wedge)** — the Stripe move. Launch to 12 hand-picked devs building AI agents for healthcare/construction/botany. If 12 devs can't stop talking about it, we've won the tastemakers of the next decade.

**Gut:** We probably need to run all three in parallel and see which one catches fire first, but the Stripe-move is the most under-utilized in our current plan. Everyone who builds a vertical AI agent in the next 12 months should want to be on our MCP server. **This is probably the single most leveraged thing we're not yet doing.**

## Open question 2 — The "why can't OKG make real money" problem

Orchids are the emotional heart of the company. The Bloom Ledger is the distribution engine. Ecuagenera is the commerce partner. But orchids will never generate the $100M+ ARR that healthcare or construction will. Investors will feel this and ask "why are you spending time on this?"

**Possible answers:**
- "OKG is the architectural R&D budget. We are building the pattern where the pattern is most visible and most defensible. When it works for orchids, it works for everything."
- "OKG is the brand. People don't trust a healthcare-compliance company until they see the craft. The orchid garden is the proof that we care about beauty — which is what makes the healthcare garden feel trustworthy."
- "OKG is the press engine. The Bloom Ledger documentary gets us on the cover of Wired. That gets us in the inbox of every CMIO and hospital CEO in America. Healthcare sales cycles shorten by months."
- "OKG is the hiring engine. The engineers we need to build HKG want to work on the Sound Garden and the Mind Garden, not on credentialing workflows. Without the meaning gardens we can't recruit the talent to build the cash gardens."

All four answers are correct. We should lead with whichever resonates most with a given investor.

## Open question 3 — The Machine Lane as a standalone company?

This is the scariest question in the whole strategy. What if the Machine Lane is so valuable that it should be spun out as its own company? "The MCP server for industry knowledge — one API, twelve verticals, every AI agent on earth connects here."

**Arguments for keeping it inside:**
- The gardens feed the graph which powers the MCP server. Separating them breaks the network effects.
- The brand story is cleaner: one company, one pattern, four lanes.
- It's a long-term moat, not a near-term product. Spin-out would be premature.

**Arguments for spinning out:**
- Different buyer (AI developers, not healthcare / construction / botanical stakeholders).
- Different pricing model (usage-based API, not SaaS).
- Different recruiting pool (infra engineers vs. full-stack product engineers).
- If AWS had stayed inside Amazon-the-retailer, it would have been a smaller business.

**My current view:** Keep it inside for now but **explicitly treat it as its own business unit** with its own P&L, its own roadmap, and its own messaging. Revisit spin-out question when Machine Lane ARR crosses $10M.

## Open question 5 — The "XR Workers / Knowledge Gardens" brand relationship

How does Knowledge Gardens relate to the broader XR Workers umbrella (MythOS, XR Studio, Game Layer, SignalOps)?

**Option A: Knowledge Gardens is the product, XR Workers is the studio.** XR Workers is the creative/technical studio that builds Knowledge Gardens for different verticals. Knowledge Gardens is what we sell. XR Workers is what's behind the curtain. (Think: Pixar is the studio, Toy Story is the product.)

**Option B: XR Workers is the product, Knowledge Gardens is the first product line.** XR Workers is the parent company. Knowledge Gardens is the flagship. MythOS, XR Studio, Game Layer, SignalOps are sister product lines. (Think: Alphabet is the parent, Google is the flagship.)

**Option C: Knowledge Gardens absorbs XR Workers entirely.** The XR Workers brand is retired. Knowledge Gardens becomes the sole company identity. (Cleanest story for investors.)

**Current lean:** Option A for the seed raise. Knowledge Gardens is the investable thing. XR Workers is mentioned in the founder-bio section as "the studio that built it." The four pillars are future optionality, not current pitch material.

The Bloom Ledger documentary is critical to OKG distribution. But it requires Chilly to be in Ecuador shooting. Every week Chilly is filming is a week Chilly is not building product.

## Open question 8 — How do we actually measure AI citation share?

The AI Citation Moat is supposedly a huge part of our defensibility. But **how do we know if it's working?** What's the measurement plan?

Rough ideas:
- Track `User-Agent` strings on entity page requests — count LLM crawlers.
- Set up a standard set of "test queries" per garden and run them weekly against Claude, GPT, Gemini, Perplexity. Count how many return us in the answer or citations.
- Monitor `llms.txt` request volume.
- Referrer tracking on clicks back from AI chatbot interfaces.

This should probably be its own dashboard — the "Citation Observatory" — and it should be public so investors can see it in real time during the raise.
